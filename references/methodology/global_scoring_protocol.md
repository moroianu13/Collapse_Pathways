# Global Scoring Protocol

This protocol explains how to assign present-day global scores for the Global Historical Pattern Overlap / Fragility Index.
It is designed for cautious manual scoring using public news, institutional indicators, and other reputable contemporary evidence.

This is **not** a collapse prediction protocol.
It is a structured way to estimate how strongly current global conditions overlap with historically recurrent fragility patterns found in the historical dataset.

## Core Rule

Each factor should be scored on a `0` to `3` scale:

- `0 = absent / negligible`
- `1 = low`
- `2 = moderate`
- `3 = high`

## General Scoring Standard

When assigning a score, consider all five of these dimensions together:

- **Geographic spread**: Is the issue localized, cross-regional, or global?
- **Persistence over time**: Is it short-lived, recurring, or sustained over months/years?
- **Severity**: Are effects limited, serious, or system-disruptive?
- **Systemic spillover potential**: Could it propagate across institutions, markets, food systems, security systems, or political orders?
- **Agreement across multiple reputable sources**: Do several credible sources point in the same direction, or is the evidence mixed?

A factor should generally move from low toward high only when more than one of these dimensions strengthens at the same time.

## How To Use Public Evidence

Use a mix of:

- reputable international news outlets
- multilateral institutions
- statistical agencies
- research institutes
- financial or commodity monitoring sources

Prefer convergence across multiple source types.
If the evidence is mixed, fast-moving, or politically contested, keep the score more conservative and lower the confidence level.

## Score-Level Guidance

### Score 0

Use `0` when the factor is absent, negligible, or too minor to matter at a global-system level.
Short-lived, localized, or low-impact events that do not create meaningful systemic stress usually belong here.

### Score 1

Use `1` when the factor is visible but limited.
This usually means constrained geographic spread, manageable severity, weak spillover, or inconsistent evidence across sources.

### Score 2

Use `2` when the factor is clearly present at a meaningful global level.
This usually means broad spread, sustained persistence, notable severity, and plausible cross-system spillovers supported by multiple reputable sources.

### Score 3

Use `3` when the factor is strongly visible across the global system and is producing severe, persistent, and systemically significant stress.
This should normally require wide geographic relevance, strong spillover potential, and broad agreement across multiple reputable sources.

## Factor-Specific Guidance

### `legitimacy_crisis`

Score this based on whether major governing institutions across multiple world regions are widely seen as ineffective, distrusted, or unable to command compliance.
Low means visible but contained trust erosion; moderate means broad cross-regional distrust and repeated legitimacy disputes; high means deep, widespread crises of governing authority with clear effects on compliance, elections, regime stability, or institutional functioning.

### `geopolitical_instability`

Score this based on the extent to which interstate tensions, territorial disputes, wars, or coercive power shifts are destabilizing the international system.
Low means regional instability with limited broader consequences; moderate means multiple serious theaters or recurring escalation risks; high means widespread instability with major strategic spillovers for trade, alliances, deterrence, or borders.

### `adaptive_capacity_resilience`

This is a resilience factor, not a stress factor.
Score it based on how well major global institutions, states, and coordination mechanisms appear able to absorb shocks, reroute stress, and restore functionality.
Low means weak buffering capacity and repeated failure to adapt; moderate means partial but uneven adaptation; high means strong, credible, and repeated evidence of successful adjustment, coordination, and recovery.

### `political_fragmentation_polarization`

Score this based on whether major political systems are increasingly fractured, polarized, or unable to sustain working coalitions and coherent governance.
Low means polarization is visible but still governable; moderate means fragmentation repeatedly blocks decision-making across multiple important systems; high means severe political breakdown, coalition failure, or sustained inability to produce stable governing settlements across many regions.

### `trade_disruption`

Score this based on disruption to trade routes, shipping, strategic supply chains, finance-linked exchange systems, or major commercial corridors.
Low means limited interruption or temporary rerouting; moderate means repeated disruption across key sectors or routes; high means severe, persistent, and globally consequential disruption with major spillovers into inflation, shortages, production, or strategic rivalry.

### `conflict_pressure`

Score this based on the burden that war, militarization, armed conflict risk, and security competition place on the global system.
Low means conflict pressure is contained; moderate means conflict burdens are broad and persistent across several theaters; high means sustained conflict pressure is consuming state capacity, increasing escalation risk, and reinforcing systemic instability.

### `fiscal_strain`

Score this based on whether major states and institutions are under mounting debt, deficit, financing, or budgetary stress that materially weakens policy flexibility.
Low means strain exists but remains manageable; moderate means fiscal pressure is widespread and increasingly constraining; high means severe and persistent fiscal stress is limiting state response capacity across multiple major economies or systems.

### `urban_social_stress`

Score this based on concentrated social stress in large urban systems, including unrest, inequality-linked instability, service strain, or severe social pressure in dense population centers.
Low means localized episodes or manageable stress; moderate means recurring multi-city or multi-country strain with political effects; high means broad and persistent urban instability with clear implications for governance, public order, or social cohesion.

### `institutional_rigidity`

Score this based on whether major institutions are failing to adjust to new stresses because of procedural rigidity, bureaucratic inertia, ideological lock-in, or coordination failure.
Low means institutions are slow but still adaptable; moderate means repeated evidence of delayed or inadequate adjustment; high means institutions are visibly unable to adapt in ways that amplify rather than reduce systemic stress.

### `food_insecurity`

Score this based on the scale, persistence, and spread of food stress affecting populations, supply systems, or affordability.
Low means food insecurity is serious but regionally concentrated; moderate means multiple regions face sustained pressure with spillovers through prices, migration, or unrest; high means broad global food stress is materially threatening social stability and cross-system resilience.

### `resource_dependency`

Score this based on dependence on fragile energy, mineral, commodity, water, or supply inputs where disruption would produce wide systemic consequences.
Low means dependencies are real but diversified or buffered; moderate means multiple critical dependencies look vulnerable; high means key global systems are heavily exposed to narrow, fragile, or geopolitically risky resource channels.

### `environmental_stress`

Score this based on the degree to which climate, ecological, drought, flood, heat, or broader environmental stress is producing current global-system pressure.
Low means environmental stress is visible but limited in systemic reach; moderate means repeated and cross-regional environmental shocks are materially affecting food, infrastructure, migration, or governance; high means environmental stress is persistent, severe, and strongly amplifying fragility across multiple global systems at once.

## Confidence Guidance

For each factor, assign a confidence level alongside the score:

- `low`: evidence is mixed, sparse, fast-changing, or contested
- `medium`: evidence is fairly consistent, but not fully settled
- `high`: multiple reputable sources strongly agree and the signal is clear

Confidence should reflect the quality and convergence of evidence, not the seriousness of the factor itself.

## Good Practice

- Write short evidence notes for every score.
- Prefer conservative scores when evidence is mixed.
- Revisit scores periodically rather than reacting to single headlines.
- Keep a dated audit trail if you update scores over time.

## Interpretation Reminder

The final index should be interpreted as a measure of **historical pattern overlap**.
It does **not** show collapse probability, does **not** provide a forecast, and should not be used to support sensational claims.
