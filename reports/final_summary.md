# Final Summary

## Historical Collapse-Pattern Findings

This project codes 15 historical collapse or severe-transformation cases into a shared comparative framework. The dataset is designed to capture patterns across political, social, economic, environmental, military, and institutional dimensions while keeping uncertainty explicit. It is best understood as a structured first-pass literature-based research instrument rather than a final historical verdict.

Across the current dataset, the strongest differentiators between lower-collapse rows and higher-collapse rows are:

- `territorial_loss`
- `political_fragmentation`
- `legitimacy_crisis`
- `fiscal_crisis`
- `urban_decline`
- `civil_war_internal_conflict`

Terminal-collapse rows are especially associated with very high `political_fragmentation`, `territorial_loss`, `fiscal_crisis`, `legitimacy_crisis`, `administrative_overload`, and `institutional_rigidity`.

## Uncertainty-Aware Interpretation

The dataset intentionally treats uncertainty as information rather than noise. Factors coded `9` are not missing by accident; they indicate that the evidence is insufficient, contested, weakly period-specific, or not clearly applicable.

The most uncertainty-limited factors across the dataset are `temperature_anomaly`, `flood_environmental_shock`, `inflation_currency_instability`, `ethnic_sectarian_fragmentation`, `ecological_degradation`, and `demographic_pressure`. The most uncertain cases remain Bronze Age Collapse States, Easter Island, Maya, Classic Mesopotamian States, and Inca Empire.

This means stronger conclusions should rely more heavily on patterns that remain visible even after uncertainty is taken seriously.

## Present-Day Global Fragility Framework

The project extends the historical analysis into a **Global Historical Pattern Overlap / Fragility Index**. This extension does not estimate collapse probability. Instead, it measures how strongly present-day global conditions overlap with historically recurrent stress patterns using a manual scoring template and fixed historical weights.

The current baseline scoring draft produces:

- Baseline Global Fragility Index: `73.35 / 100`
- Interpretation band: `High fragility`

The strongest weighted contributors in the current baseline draft are geopolitical instability, trade disruption, conflict pressure, legitimacy crisis, and adaptive capacity / resilience. The resilience factor is included as an offset in the net-score logic rather than as an additional stress driver.

## Scenario Results

The scenario analysis keeps historical weights fixed and varies only the present-day scoring assumptions.

- Conservative scenario: `55.25` -> `Moderate fragility`
- Baseline scenario: `73.35` -> `High fragility`
- High-stress scenario: `91.45` -> `Severe systemic stress`

The scenario spread shows that the current-world extension is meaningfully sensitive to judgment-based scoring assumptions. That sensitivity is expected and is part of the point of the scenario framework.

## Conclusion

At this stage, the project supports a cautious but meaningful conclusion: across widely different cases, severe collapse tends to coincide less with any single universal cause than with converging clusters of political fragmentation, legitimacy erosion, territorial weakening, fiscal strain, institutional rigidity, and conflict pressure. Environmental and demographic stresses matter in some cases, but their evidentiary coverage is more uneven and case-dependent.

The present-day extension suggests that current global conditions overlap substantially with several historically recurrent fragility patterns, but this should be interpreted as an exploratory historical comparison rather than a predictive claim. The project is most useful as a transparent comparative framework for identifying patterns, testing interpretations, and guiding later human review.
